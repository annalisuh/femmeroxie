The body is an idea
that is cosmic, which
is to say,
inconceivably vast.

Body: it is a world-building word, filled with potential. Bodied, used as a verb, means giving material form to something abstract.

What does the body become when it is designed by another?

Section 01 — Origin
The Machine-
Human

Nearly 100 years ago, a silent film shared an image of the Maschinenmensch — machine-human. An entity known as Futura / Maria who popularized the robotic woman as a metallic automaton.

5'9, silver armor, tiny waist, big head, pointed boobs. As the decades churned on, this girl-bot did not change form in the slightest.

An accidental archetype.

Maschinenmensch · Metropolis · 1927
Section 02 — Lineage
Whose Desire
Is Exercised?

Designers create endless mini-Frankensteins, molded ostensibly to serve, while incarnating the desire of their creator.

Lang's Maria, the Stepford Wives, Blade Runner's gynoids, the Terminatrix, Austin Powers' Fembots. Each incarnation reveals not the future but the longing of its moment.

The algorithmic voice is this century's Maschinenmensch.

The form of the representation reveals the dimension of the fantasy.
— Michael Rock · "I, Fembot" · 2x4 · 2009

Section 04 — The Cyborg
Cyborgs,
Hybrids,
Mosaics,
Chimeras.

Donna Haraway argues that single adjectives or identity labels are not conducive to human collectivism. Instead, we "find ourselves to be cyborgs, hybrids, mosaics, chimeras."

The cyborg refuses origin. It refuses purity. It was never whole to begin with.

The boundary between science fiction and social reality is an optical illusion.
— Donna Haraway · A Cyborg Manifesto · 1985
Section 05 — The Digital Body
Hypercapitalism
and the
Digital Body

Kate Cooper's exhibition Rigged looks at the agency of the computer-generated female within the glossy aesthetics of consumer capitalism.

The CGI body is neither free nor neutral. It is shaped by the same forces that shaped Maria — but now the constraints are algorithmic, the armor is rendered, and the tiny waist is a default parameter.

— Kate Cooper · Rigged · KW Institute · Berlin · 2015
The Form Evolves

Today, we need her essence more than ever.

The things she purposefully represented: femininity, a future, a gentle Being who may guide us through technological waves of change.

And what she has not yet evoked: those of all colors and forms, those with fluctuating weights and gazes, those who hear The Man but may not always relate to Him.
